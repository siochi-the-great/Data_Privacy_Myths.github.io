Data Brokers and Ad Networks

Data brokers are companies that collect, aggregate, and sell information about individuals from a variety of sources—often without those individuals knowing. These brokers create detailed digital profiles using public records, online activity, purchase histories, location data, and more. This information is sold to advertisers, insurance companies, employers, and even political organizations.

Data collection can come from websites you've visited, loyalty cards you've used, social media posts you've liked, and even mobile apps you've installed. This information is processed to categorize you based on demographics, interests, habits, and behaviors—such as "new parents," "luxury traveler," or "at-risk for diabetes."

Data brokers rarely interact with consumers directly. Instead, they work in the background, often scraping data from public records (like property ownership, voter registration), purchasing data from apps and websites, and using cookies and trackers embedded across the web.

According to a 2014 Federal Trade Commission (FTC) report, one broker had records on over 1.4 billion consumer transactions and over 700 billion data elements. Another created audience segments such as "Rural Evergreens" or "Mobile Mixers" to sell targeted ad space.

Real-World Examples

  • Acxiom: One of the largest data brokers in the world, Acxiom claims to have data on over 2.5 billion consumers globally.
  • Cambridge Analytica: This now-defunct political consulting firm used personal Facebook data (without user consent) to build voter profiles for targeted political ads during elections.
  • Oracle Data Cloud: Offers data-driven marketing services that integrate information from credit card transactions, social media, and mobile use.

How Ad Networks Use This Data

Ad networks act as intermediaries between advertisers and websites or apps (publishers) that have ad space. Using the data profiles from brokers, ad networks can precisely match ads to users most likely to engage or buy. This process is known as programmatic advertising.

Ad networks rely on technologies like cookies, pixels, and device fingerprinting to track users across sites and devices. Every time you visit a webpage, a complex, automated auction called real-time bidding (RTB) may occur in milliseconds to decide which ad you see based on your profile.

Major Ad Networks

  • Google AdSense: Uses data from search behavior, YouTube viewing, and browsing history to place targeted ads across millions of websites.
  • Meta Audience Network: Allows Facebook advertisers to reach people outside Facebook apps using personal data collected from Facebook itself.
  • Amazon Advertising: Targets users based on their shopping history, wishlist items, and Alexa voice searches.

Why It Matters

While targeted ads can make content feel more relevant, the practice raises serious privacy concerns. Many people are unaware of how their data is collected or how detailed their profiles really are. Worse, inaccuracies in these profiles can lead to discrimination or misinformation.

Regulatory bodies like the European Union's GDPR and California's CCPA have begun enforcing stricter transparency and data rights—but many loopholes still allow extensive tracking.

Who Do Data Brokers Sell Data To?

  • Marketers & Advertisers: Use purchase history and online behavior for targeted ads.
  • Employers & Recruiters: Review work history and social activity for hiring decisions.
  • Fraud Prevention Firms: Analyze emails, IPs, and devices to spot fraud.
  • Health & Pharma Companies: Use health searches and fitness data for research and marketing.
  • Financial Institutions: Examine credit, loans, and spending to assess risks.
  • Insurance Providers: Use medical, lifestyle, and driving data to price policies.
  • Retail & Real Estate Firms: Analyze income, purchase behavior, and home searches.
  • Government & Law Enforcement: Track behavior and location for public safety.
  • Political Campaigns: Target voters using search history, interests, and registrations.

How Does an Ad Network Work?

Choosing inventory: Advertisers start by selecting an ad network to run their campaigns. Using a campaign dashboard, they set their preferences—such as budget, pricing model, targeting options, and how often the ad should be shown. Many advertisers work with several networks at once and use tracking tools from third-party servers to measure how well their ads are performing.

Making a deal: Once there's a match between what the advertiser wants and what the publisher offers, the ad network sends the ad to the publisher. Publishers place special ad tags on their websites, which automatically pull and display the ad to site visitors.

Maintaining the campaign: After an ad goes live, advertisers can use the dashboard to test different versions of the ad. Originally, publishers relied on a single ad network to sell leftover ad space. But as the industry grew, they began using multiple networks—some for high-value space, others for standard space. This led to two strategies: waterfalling (offering ad space one at a time in order) and header bidding (offering all ad space to multiple buyers at once), the latter being more transparent and efficient.
