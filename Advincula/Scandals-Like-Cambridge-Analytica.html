Scandals Like Cambridge Analytica

The Cambridge Analytica scandal entered the global spotlight five years ago, shaking the political and tech worlds alike. It brought the issues of political persuasion and data mining uncomfortably close to every Facebook user. In its aftermath, the scandal sparked widespread debate about the ethics of data privacy and the growing influence of social media on electoral politics. Even today, Cambridge Analytica continues to shape conversations around digital democracy and data governance.

Anatomy of Scandal

Cambridge Analytica was founded in 2013, but the roots of its microtargeting capabilities trace back to Facebook’s 2010 rollout of the Open Graph platform. This update included a new version of Facebook’s API—a tool that allows one application to access the data or features of another. With this API, developers could view users’ social connections and identify shared interests and likes.

Open Graph was widely used in both political and non-political contexts. For example, President Obama’s campaign built an app that helped connect known supporters with potential ones, leveraging mutual friendships or common interests—such as liking the same sports team. Outside politics, games like Farmville used the API to show players which of their friends were also playing, enhancing social interaction within the app.

The API also had powerful applications in political research. Aleksander Kogan, a researcher at the University of Cambridge, used it to collect data on Facebook users and study behavioral patterns. He later shared this data with Cambridge Analytica, which claimed it could use such information to help political campaigns deliver precisely tailored messages—a practice known as “microtargeting.”

Effects of the Scandal

In the years following the Cambridge Analytica scandal, the fallout spurred legislative action around the world—most notably in the European Union, with the introduction of the Digital Services Act and the Digital Markets Act. The scandal ignited global debate over the ethics of microtargeting in political advertising. In response, several platforms took steps to limit or ban political ad targeting: Twitter banned political ads altogether, Google reduced its targeting capabilities, and Facebook introduced an option for users to opt out of seeing political ads. Additionally, platforms like Facebook and Google developed transparency tools to allow users to view all active campaign ads.

Legislative momentum continues in the EU, where proposals are underway that could significantly restrict the use of microtargeting for political campaigns. Meanwhile, legal challenges persist. Most recently, Facebook agreed to a $725 million settlement related to the scandal.

The Cambridge Analytica controversy had a lasting impact on data privacy, political campaigning, and the role of social media in democratic processes. It pushed governments to enact stricter data protection laws and forced companies to reconsider how they collect and use personal data. While Facebook has implemented new privacy measures, these changes have come with trade-offs—particularly for researchers, who now face more restrictions when trying to access platform data for academic purposes, potentially affecting efforts toward transparency and accountability.
