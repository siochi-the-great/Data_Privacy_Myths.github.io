Private messages aren’t always fully private. While end-to-end encryption protects messages from being read by outsiders, not all messaging apps use this by default. Companies may access unencrypted messages or metadata (like who you messaged and when). Governments can request data through legal channels, and some companies may comply, especially if required by law. If privacy is a concern, use apps that offer strong end-to-end encryption and have a clear, transparent privacy policy.

Metadata Collection

  • Even with E2EE, messaging platforms may collect metadata—information about who you communicate with, when, and how often. This data can be accessed by companies and, under certain circumstances, by governments.

Legal Requests

  • Governments can compel companies to provide user data through legal means. For instance, a report highlighted that 66% of global government requests for user data from major tech companies like Microsoft, Meta, Google, and Apple are fulfilled.

Government Surveillance Grows:

From 2013 to 2019, government requests for user data from tech companies like Google, Facebook, Apple, and Microsoft increased by 213%. These requests ask companies to share user information, and in many cases, the companies do—either fully or partially.

In 2013, there were just over 100,000 approved data requests. By 2019, that number had grown to nearly 500,000. Microsoft and Facebook had the highest numbers of disclosed requests. This shows that even if you're using secure platforms, governments can still access data if they go through legal channels.
