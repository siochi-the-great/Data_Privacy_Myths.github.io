Debunking Location Myths

Let’s bust some of the most common misconceptions people have about how phones and apps track location.

Myth #1: "Turning off GPS disables all tracking"

Fact: Turning off GPS only stops satellite-based tracking. Wi-Fi networks, Bluetooth beacons, IP addresses, and cell towers can still locate your device. [Quartz, 2018]

Myth #2: "Airplane Mode guarantees privacy"

Fact: Airplane Mode disables most radios but not sensors like accelerometers or gyroscopes, which can still infer motion and routes. [University of Illinois, Subway Tracking Study]

Myth #3: "Private/Incognito mode hides my location"

Fact: Incognito only stops your browser from saving history. Your IP address, device fingerprint, and geolocation APIs are still visible to websites. [Mozilla Developer Docs]

Myth #4: "Apps only track you when open"

Fact: Many apps track you in the background—even when closed—by using system-level permissions and background services. [NYT Privacy Project, 2018]

Myth #5: "If I deny location access, the app can't know where I am"

Fact: Apps can still infer your location through your IP address, nearby Wi-Fi, Bluetooth proximity, or motion patterns even if GPS is off. [Electronic Frontier Foundation]

Myth #6: "Only tech companies care about my location"

Fact: Your location data is often sold to data brokers, advertisers, insurance companies, and even law enforcement—without your full awareness. [The Markup, 2021]

Myth #7: "Tracking stops when you delete an app"

Fact: If the app synced data to a cloud service or third party, your data may still exist elsewhere—and uninstalling doesn’t remove previous logs. [Android Developer Docs]

Myth #8: "iPhones are safe from location tracking"

Fact: Apple limits background access better than Android, but apps with granted permissions can still collect and transmit your data. No device is 100% immune. [Apple Transparency Report]

Takeaways

  • GPS is only one of many tracking signals your phone emits.
  • Turning off features doesn’t erase past data or hidden tracking tools.
  • Awareness of app permissions and sensors is essential to privacy.
  • Both iPhones and Androids have privacy gaps that users must address manually.
